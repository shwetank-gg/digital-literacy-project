Do's:





Don't make them guess: Use a subject line that acts like a movie trailer—give them the highlights so they know exactly what they’re opening.



Start with a warm hello: Think of your greeting as a digital handshake. A simple "Dear Professor Sharma" shows you’re approaching the conversation with respect.



Respect their clock: Get to the "why" of your email early on. Most people appreciate it when you share your main point first and save the deep details for the middle.



Mind the "vibe": Since tone can be hard to read over text, aim for a blend of polite and professional. It’s always better to be a little too formal than accidentally rude.



Stick the landing: Wrap things up with a simple, clean sign-off like "Best" or "Sincerely." It’s the best way to leave a great final impression.





Don'ts:



Use no casual language or slang (avoid “Hey” or “BTW”).



Don’t publish a headline that says nothing or “Zhao said”.



Every paragraph should be cleanly written. Reading long paragraphs can make your mail hard to understand.



Also, don't miss early newsletters since they provide a treasure trove of educational materials that you can use with your team for long periods with no resource overhead.



The most important thing is to escape the fast food culture of luxury hotels in China.



At the same time, don’t be afraid to ask any other questions you can think of about The Rise Of Chinese Restaurants.
